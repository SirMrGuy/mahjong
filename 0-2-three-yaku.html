The Big Three Yaku

The First Step to Winning

It's technically possible to win a mahjong game without actually winning any hands,[2] and it's definitely plausible to sneak away with second or third as such. Nevertheless, it's clear that we want to win hands when we can.

Any winning hand requires a yaku (役). Almost every hand contains one of the following three:

The Big Three Yaku[4]
Yaku Japanese English Literal Frequency
Riichi 立直/リーチ Riichi/Reach Straight upright 2 in 5
Yakuhai 役牌 Dragon or Wind Value tile 2 in 5
Tanyao(chuu) 断幺(九) All Simples Broken ones (and nines) 1 in 5

These yaku fulfill largely complementary roles: riichi is only possible with closed hands, while yakuhai and tanyao are mutually exclusive. You should be declaring riichi on most of your close hands for reasons we will discuss in the next chapter. As for open hands, yakuhai and tanyao dominate: the next three most common yaku that are available for open hands (in order honitsu, sanshoku doujun, and toitoi) combine for around just one in ten wins, and many of the former two are achieved with closed hands.

How does this impact our strategy? One of the first considerations when we receive our starting hand should be to decide which yaku to pursue. Because of the primacy of these three yaku, in most cases they should be the first things we think of, and that will help guide the development of the hand.
