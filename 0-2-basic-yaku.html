It's technically possible to win a mahjong game without actually winning any hands,[1] and it's definitely plausible to sneak away with second or third as such. Nevertheless, we want to win hands when we can.

Any winning hand requires a yaku (役). Almost every hand contains one of the following three:

Basic Yaku[3]
Yaku Japanese English Literal Frequency
Riichi 立直/リーチ Riichi/Reach Straight upright 2 in 5
Yakuhai 役牌 Dragon or Wind Value tile 2 in 5
Tanyao(chuu) 断幺(九) All Simples Broken ones (and nines) 1 in 5

These yaku fulfill largely complementary roles: riichi is only possible with closed hands, while yakuhai and tanyao are mutually exclusive. You should be declaring riichi on most of your closed hands for reasons we will discuss in later chapters. As for open hands, yakuhai and tanyao dominate: the next three most common yaku that are available for open hands (in order honitsu, sanshoku doujun, and toitoi) combine for around just one in ten wins, and many of the former two are achieved with closed hands.

Already with these three yaku we can start to see the common tradeoff between hand speed and value. In particular, unless you get lucky and draw quite a few dora, open yakuhai and open tanyao hands will tend to be fairly cheap compared to riichi hands. However, they make up for this with speed, often allowing you to complete hands much more quickly, essentially by expanding the set of tiles that are available to you to include some of your opponents' discards. Keeping a hand closed and hoping for riichi is comparatively slower, and thus will make you win fewer hands, but generally is the best way to shoot for very large wins.

How does this impact our strategy? One of the first considerations when we receive our starting hand should be to decide which yaku to pursue. Because of the primacy of these three yaku, in most cases they should be the first things we think of, and that will help guide the development of the hand.
