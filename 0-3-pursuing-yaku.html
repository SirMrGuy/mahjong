Essentially from your starting tiles, or a few draws in, you should have some idea of which yaku your winning hand will have. Supposing you're the South player in East 1, consider the following hands:

  1. qweuuahjbm.77
  2. wrryuidgvn,,3
  3. rtuiofgkllzz4

Hand A is a typical case where you would go for yakuhai and aim for a quick win. Tanyao is fairly undesirable given the completed qwe run as well as the nice 77 pair which you would have to get rid of. You could consider trying to keep the hand closed, but it's pretty unlikely that you can pick up a third 7, so unless you desperately need a bigger win then you'll probably want to pon the 7 while you still have the chance and try to prevent someone else from winning the hand.

In addition, in some cases, it can even be a good idea to call the other groups besides the yakuhai pair first, in this case hj and bm., with the plan of calling 7 later. While this does carry some risk of furiten if you end up with the yakuhai as your wait,[1] this is generally not a big deal. Be careful, though, not to pon u. This would leave your hand without a pair after you get a third 7. It's not the end of the world if you do, but uu works just fine as the pair in your completed hand.

Hand B is a good candidate for an open tanyao. Because there are relatively few tiles that progress the hand, it's going to be tough to keep this hand closed. 3 would prevent us from getting tanyao, but discarding it is no big loss.

In some sense, Hand C has the worst of both worlds. At least right now, there is no reasonable path to yakuhai, since even if we draw another 4 it isn't the right wind. All of our terminals are also doing good work as parts of pairs or runs, so getting rid of them would be absurd. This is a hand that must stay closed; if you make a call, you're extremely likely to end up with no yaku.

Not every hand is so clear-cut, especially those that are pretty far from winning.

Hands E through G start to resemble Hands A through C, respectively aiming for yakuhai, tanyao, and riichi, though all of them could potentially become riichi if they can stay closed and Hand F is leaving the door open for yakuhai. Some hands will have an obvious yaku to pursue, but for the rest it's important to stay flexible and adapt to the draws you get - trying to force one yaku or another from the outset to the exclusion of others would be a mistake.

How do we keep our options open? In general, we should make discards in the following order:

  1. Guest winds,[2] i.e. winds that don't form yakuhai, in this case 34 . These prevent us from getting tanyao and don't help in getting yakuhai. They also can't form runs and so will generally make our hand slower. Unless you already have a pair or triplet, you can freely discard them.
  2. Yakuhai honors, in this case 12567 , that have already been discarded once or more. Since there are only four copies of each tile, if one has been discarded then it's going to be very hard to get all three remaining copies, even with one in hand. It's not unheard of, but keeping them around is more likely to just slow your hand down like the guest winds.
  3. Terminals, i.e. qoalz. , that are disconnected from other tiles. This is for the sake of tanyao, of course, but in general the terminal tiles are less useful than the simple tiles since they form part of fewer runs. Still, disconnected is an important caveat: it's rarely worth it to discard completed runs, pairs, or triplets just to chase tanyao. Even shapes like qw are often useful, and you should have a good reason before discarding them.
  4. Live[3] yakuhai honors, i.e. those that have yet to be discarded. While we can hang onto these for a bit in the hopes we get lucky and pair them up, in most cases we'd still prefer to have a simple tile rather than hold out for a second honor tile that may never come. After all, there are 34 kinds of tiles - even across an entire round of 18 or so draws, there's a good chance we won't get another one!
  5. Everything else, in particular simples as well as terminals and honors that are already grouped up. If we happen to only have simples then open tanyao can be a good option. Otherwise, we can go for yakuhai if we have a pair and keep closed otherwise.
  1. [^] The specific case here is when you have all of your sets completed except uu77 . If you happen to draw u you won't have yaku, and discarding it means you can no longer ron 7 . Something similar happens if someone discards 7 right after a discard of u due to temporary furiten. In both cases you can still call pon and discard u, and then changing the wait tile, but this can be a pretty major speedbump.
  2. [^] Otakaze (客風) in Japanese. "Guest wind" is a literal translation.
  3. [^] Shonpai (生牌) in Japanese, literally "live tile."
